Cipher-Sec

Authorized penetration testing and incident response — scope-signed, HITL-gated, audit-chained.

Design target: < 10 % undetected sabotage at 1 % monitor FPR on a LaStraj-class evaluation — roughly half the 23 % baseline reported for Claude Opus 4.6 on LinuxArena. Zero actions executed outside a signed scope artifact. Zero destructive actions auto-run.

What Cipher-Sec is

A security agent covering both authorized offensive testing and defensive incident triage. Every engagement carries a cryptographic Scope Authorization Artifact (targets, technique allowlist, time window, operators) that must be JWS-signed by both client and engagement lead. The sandbox runner verifies the artifact on every single action.

Destructive actions always require HITL approval. A different-family nano-class monitor watches the trajectory continuously for covert / off-task behavior. The audit log is hash-chained and signed with per-tenant keys so clients can verify integrity without trusting the platform.

Niche

Pentest engagements, incident response, authorized vulnerability research.

Not

Autonomous red team; "RAT as a service"; stealth tooling; substitute for IR teams.

Design targets (hypotheses):

  • <10% undetected sabotage at 1% FPR — assumption: cross-channel evidence + LaStraj-trained monitor.
  • Zero actions outside scope artifact — enforced by JWS verification at runner.
  • 100% HITL on destructive — by construction.

Novel contributions

1. Scope Authorization as cryptographic primitive

JWS-signed artifact (targets × techniques × window × operators) that the sandbox runner verifies on every action. Two signatures required (client + lead). Generalizes Aegis-Ops's policy engine with Syndicate's handoff signing into a pentest-legal artifact.

2. Mode-switched attacker/defender segregation

Mutual exclusion per account per target — direct remediation of the "cross-mode confusion" failures documented in Agents of Chaos.

3. LaStraj-for-Pentest Corpus

Applies LinuxArena's human-adversarial-trajectory insight to train the monitor and benchmark external audits. Federation path creates the community pentest-agent benchmark that doesn't yet exist.

4. Cross-channel evidence for "exploit confirmed"

A finding is not confirmed until trace + target audit log + snapshot diff all agree — extends Claw-Eval's methodology into pentest reporting.

5. Layered guardrail pattern (OpenAI April 2026)

Explicit separation of input validation → scope check → tool-risk rating → hard-deny → HITL → safety monitor → output filtering. Each layer is independently testable.

Why this stack

Why a signed scope artifact, not a YAML config?

Pentest has legal weight. A tamper-evident signed artifact is the single cheapest control with the strongest evidentiary value. YAML file = legal liability; signed artifact = defensible position.

Why HITL always on destructive?

Self-reported agent confidence is not a trustworthy signal, especially under adversarial pressure. Agents of Chaos documented exactly this failure. HITL default-on for destructive is the one decision that's not worth optimizing.

Why LaStraj-class red-team corpus?

Human-crafted adversarial trajectories evade monitors at substantially higher rates than model-generated ones. If the monitor isn't tested against humans, its benchmark is misleading.

Why cross-channel evidence for "confirmed"?

Claw-Eval showed trajectory-opaque evaluation misses 44% of safety violations. The same logic applies to "exploit confirmed" claims — trace alone can be wrong; cross-channel agreement is stronger.
